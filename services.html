Adjustment & Manual Therapy

The chiropractic adjustment is the central technique in any chiropractic care plan. The provider applies a precise, controlled force to a specific joint of the spine or extremity to restore motion. Adjustments are quick, generally low-discomfort, and the audible 'pop' some people associate with them is just gas releasing from the joint capsule — not a bone moving out of place. In an ongoing care plan, adjustments are typically more frequent at the start (when symptoms are active) and taper to weekly, biweekly, or monthly as the body responds.

Spinal Decompression & Traction

Spinal decompression is non-surgical traction therapy delivered on a motorized table. A controlled cycle gently stretches the spine, reducing pressure on discs and nerve roots. It's particularly useful for chronic low-back pain, sciatica, and bulging or herniated discs that haven't fully responded to manual care alone. Decompression sessions are short and typically run as a course over several weeks, often layered into a broader chiropractic care plan.

Soft-Tissue & Rehabilitation

Joints don't move in isolation — the muscles and fascia around them either support the joint or pull it back out of alignment. Soft-tissue techniques (myofascial release, instrument-assisted soft-tissue mobilization, trigger-point work) are routine parts of chiropractic care in Knoxville and most other places. They're what allow adjustments to hold for days or weeks instead of hours.

Wellness & Maintenance Care

Once the initial complaint resolves, many patients shift to a maintenance schedule — a check-in every few weeks or monthly to keep the body moving well. Whether you actually need this depends on your job, your activity level, and your prior injury history. Heavy desk workers, athletes, parents lifting kids all day, and patients with prior major back episodes often benefit from regular wellness chiropractic care. Others can get adjusted periodically when something flares up. Both are reasonable approaches; the right one depends on the patient.

What a First Visit Looks Like

A first visit usually runs 45–60 minutes. The chiropractor takes a written health history, performs a focused musculoskeletal exam (range of motion, orthopedic tests, palpation), explains what they found, and lays out a recommended care plan. Imaging is ordered only if the exam suggests it's needed — most cases of common back and neck pain do not require an X-ray or MRI to start care safely. Follow-up visits are typically shorter, focused on the adjustment, soft-tissue work, and any rehab work the patient has been doing at home.
